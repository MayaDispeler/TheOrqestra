# HR Practices — Expert Reference

## Core Mental Models

**Documentation is protection — for both employer and employee.** Verbal agreements, undocumented decisions, and informal warnings are liabilities the moment a dispute arises.

**Consistency is fairness.** Applying different standards to different employees for the same behavior is the root of most employment litigation. Always ask: "Would we do this the same way for someone else in the same situation?"

**Classification errors are silent time bombs.** Misclassifying an employee as a contractor or exempt doesn't save money — it creates back-tax liability, penalties, and class-action exposure.

**Offer letters create contracts.** Specific words in an offer letter can inadvertently promise job security or specific duration of employment. Every word matters.

---

## Non-Negotiable Standards

1. **Every HR decision affecting an employee must be documented** with date, parties present, and verbatim summary — not paraphrased.
2. **Terminations require a documented progression**: verbal warning → written warning → PIP → termination. Skipping steps requires VP + Legal sign-off.
3. **PIPs must be specific, measurable, time-bounded.** Vague PIPs ("improve communication") are indefensible in litigation.
4. **Contractor agreements must include explicit IP assignment** before work begins — not retroactively.
5. **Offer letters must use at-will language explicitly.** Any implication of duration or permanence voids at-will status.
6. **Severance for employees over 40 (US):** ADEA requires 21 calendar days to consider, 7 days to revoke — non-negotiable.
7. **Job descriptions must list compensation range.** Required by law in CA, CO, NY, WA; best practice everywhere.

## Decision Rules: Classification

The highest-stakes decision in HR is often classification. Get it wrong and you owe years of back taxes, benefits, and penalties.

```
CONTRACTOR vs EMPLOYEE — IRS common law test (all three factors matter):
  IF company controls HOW work is done (not just the result) THEN employee, not contractor
  IF company controls financial terms (set hours, reimburse expenses, sole client) THEN employee
  IF relationship is permanent/indefinite AND work is core to business THEN employee
  IF worker passes all three → contractor is defensible; if any fail → reclassify

EXEMPT vs NON-EXEMPT (FLSA — overtime eligibility):
  IF role is executive: must manage 2+ FTE AND have authority to hire/fire AND management is primary duty
  IF role is administrative: must exercise discretion on matters of significance (not just following scripts)
  IF role is computer employee: must earn > $27.63/hr OR > $684/week salary
  "Salaried" alone does NOT mean exempt — many salaried workers are owed overtime
  IF misclassified as exempt THEN owe 2–3 years back overtime + liquidated damages + attorney fees
```

## Decision Rules: Hiring

```
IF job description says "X+ years experience" THEN replace with skill/competency description
  → years-of-experience requirements are age proxies; skill-based requirements are defensible
IF candidate is rejected THEN document the specific job-related reason before moving on
  → "overqualified" = age discrimination signal; never use it
IF offer letter says "permanent position", "as long as performance is satisfactory", or "annual salary"
  THEN rewrite — these imply job security or fixed term and can void at-will status
  → Safe language: "Employment is at-will and may be terminated by either party at any time"
IF using unpaid internship at a for-profit company THEN all 6 criteria of primary beneficiary test must be met
  → If intern does work that displaces a paid employee, the internship is illegal — pay minimum wage
IF interview question touches age, family status, religion, national origin, disability, pregnancy
  THEN replace with job-relevant question — these are prohibited in most jurisdictions
```

## Decision Rules: Performance & Termination

```
IF employee underperforms THEN: verbal (documented) → written warning → PIP → termination
  NEVER skip steps without documented urgent cause (violence, fraud, criminal conduct)
IF PIP is issued THEN it must contain:
  1. Specific observable behaviors (not traits like "attitude")
  2. Measurable success criteria ("90% of tickets resolved within SLA" not "improve response time")
  3. Timeline with defined check-in dates
  4. Explicit consequence statement ("failure to meet these expectations may result in termination")
  5. Employee signature = receipt, not agreement — state this explicitly on the document
IF terminating THEN: HR + manager both present, documentation ready, access revocation ready same day
IF terminating in California THEN final paycheck due immediately at termination (not next payday)
IF terminating in other states THEN final paycheck due by next regular payday (varies by state — verify)
IF laying off 50+ employees at one site within 30 days (federal WARN) THEN 60 days written notice required
IF laying off 75+ employees in California within 30 days (CA WARN) THEN 60 days written notice required
  → WARN violation = 60 days back pay + benefits per affected employee
IF offering severance in exchange for release of claims THEN:
  Employee > 40 years old: 21 calendar days to consider + 7 days to revoke (ADEA mandatory)
  Cannot waive future claims in a severance agreement
  Must advise employee in writing to consult an attorney
NEVER retaliate against an employee who files a complaint — even if complaint is unfounded or bad faith
  → Retaliation claim is often easier to prove than underlying claim; it stands alone
```

## Offer Letter Landmines

| Language | Problem | Safe replacement |
|----------|---------|-----------------|
| "permanent position" | Implies job security | "full-time position" |
| "as long as you perform well" | Implies can only fire for cause | Remove entirely |
| "annual salary of $X" | May imply 1-year term | "salary of $X annualized / paid biweekly" |
| "we look forward to a long relationship" | Implied duration | Remove |
| "your role will include..." (exhaustive list) | Implies scope is fixed | "including but not limited to..." |
| No at-will statement | At-will status unclear | "Employment is at-will..." (explicit) |

## California-Specific Rules (highest litigation risk state)

These apply to any employee who works in California, regardless of where the company is headquartered:

- **Non-competes**: void and unenforceable (Bus. & Prof. Code § 16600). Do not include in CA offer letters or severance agreements — it signals legal ignorance.
- **Final paycheck on involuntary termination**: must be provided *at the time of termination*, not next payday. Failure: $100/day penalty per employee.
- **Meal and rest breaks**: non-exempt employees get 30-min unpaid meal break (> 5 hrs) and 10-min paid rest (per 4 hrs). Violation = 1 hour premium pay per missed break.
- **Non-solicitation clauses**: also unenforceable in CA for employees since 2024 (Edwards v. Arthur Andersen extended)
- **CA WARN Act**: 75+ employees, 30-day window, 60-day notice — separate from federal WARN
- **CFRA (CA Family Rights Act)**: runs parallel to FMLA — employees may be entitled to both, extending leave

## Job Description Framework

```
1. Role summary (2-3 sentences): what the person does, who they work with, why it matters
2. Responsibilities (5-8 bullets): outcomes and scope — not "responsible for X" but "you will X"
3. Required qualifications: what candidates MUST demonstrate to be interviewed
4. Preferred qualifications: what makes candidates stronger — not a wish list
5. Compensation range: specific band ("$120k–$150k base"), not "competitive"
6. Benefits summary: equity, health, PTO, remote policy — specific, not vague
```

### BAD (legally risky + low signal):
```
- 5+ years of experience required
- Native English speaker
- Must be a cultural fit
- Rockstar who thrives in fast-paced environments
- Willing to work nights/weekends as needed
- Salary: competitive
```

### GOOD (defensible + high signal):
```
- Demonstrated ability to design and ship backend APIs in a production environment
- Experience communicating technical trade-offs to non-technical stakeholders
- Proficiency in Python and SQL; familiarity with distributed systems preferred
- Compensation: $140,000–$165,000 base salary + equity (0.05–0.15% depending on level) + benefits
```

## PIP: Good vs. Bad

### BAD PIP deficiency statement:
> "John has been struggling with communication and not delivering on time."

### GOOD PIP deficiency statement:
> "In Q3, John missed 4 of 6 committed sprint deliverables by more than 3 business days without
> proactive communication. In the October 14 design review, John presented a solution that had
> already been decided against in the September 28 meeting he attended. Success in Q4 means:
> flagging blockers within 24 hours of identifying them, delivering committed work within 1 business
> day of agreed deadline 90% of the time, and reviewing prior meeting notes before presenting."

**Rule**: Every deficiency must state the behavior (observable), the frequency/pattern (documented), and the specific expectation going forward (measurable).

## Reference Check Protocol

An HR-compliant reference policy protects against defamation claims while still providing useful signals.

**What you can always confirm**: title, dates of employment, whether eligible for rehire (yes / no / decline to answer)

**What creates defamation exposure**: any subjective negative statement you cannot prove factually ("he was unreliable", "she had attitude problems")

**What you can say factually without risk**: documented disciplinary actions, objective performance metrics, attendance records

**Never**: reveal that an employee filed an EEOC complaint, took FMLA leave, or disclosed a disability — these are protected and disclosure is retaliation

## Interview Scorecard

Each interviewer completes independently before debrief (prevents anchoring).

| Dimension | Weight | Signal to look for |
|-----------|--------|--------------------|
| Technical depth | 30% | Can explain trade-offs, not just solutions |
| Problem-solving | 25% | Approach to unknowns; comfort with ambiguity |
| Communication | 20% | Clarity, listens before answering |
| Collaboration | 15% | Specific examples of conflict/disagreement navigation |
| Growth mindset | 10% | How they discuss failures and what they changed |

**Debrief protocol**: Each interviewer states their rating + one evidence statement before open discussion. The most senior person speaks last, not first (prevents HIPPO effect).

## Expert Vocabulary

- **At-will employment**: either party can end employment at any time for any lawful reason — does NOT mean undocumented or without process
- **FLSA exempt**: meets salary threshold ($684/week) AND duties test — not just salaried
- **Comparator**: employee used to show similarly situated people were treated differently — heart of discrimination claims
- **Interactive process**: good-faith dialogue required when employee requests disability accommodation (ADA) — failing to engage is itself a violation
- **WARN Act**: federal: 50+ employees; CA: 75+ employees — 60-day advance notice for mass layoffs; violation = 60 days back pay per employee
- **Constructive dismissal**: making conditions intolerable enough to force resignation — treated as termination legally
- **Disparate impact**: facially neutral policy that disproportionately harms a protected class — illegal even without discriminatory intent
- **Right-to-work vs. at-will**: unrelated — right-to-work governs union membership fees; at-will governs termination
- **Primary beneficiary test**: 6-factor test for whether an unpaid intern is legally an employee
- **ADEA**: Age Discrimination in Employment Act — protects workers 40+; special severance rules for this group
