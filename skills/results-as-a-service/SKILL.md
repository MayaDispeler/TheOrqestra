# Results-as-a-Service Expert Reference

## Non-Negotiable Standards

1. **The outcome must be binary or objectively measurable — no ambiguity allowed.** "Value delivered" is not an outcome. "Lead booked on sales calendar" is. "Document classified with >95% confidence" is. If two parties independently measuring the same event could disagree on whether it occurred, the outcome definition is unacceptable. Resolve this in contract language before signing.
2. **Cost-per-outcome must be below 40% of price-per-outcome at steady state.** At 40% COGS ratio, you have room for infrastructure margin, support cost, and fraud/dispute losses. Above 60%, RaaS becomes loss-leading at scale. Model this unit economics calculation before committing to an outcome price — it is the business viability test.
3. **Usage events are recorded idempotently at the point of outcome occurrence.** Every outcome event carries a stable idempotency key (content-addressed hash of deterministic outcome attributes). Duplicate delivery from retries must not double-count billable events. This is a billing correctness requirement, not an optimization.
4. **The customer must have real-time visibility into outcome counts and projected invoice.** Outcome measurement opacity kills trust and creates disputes. Every RaaS customer gets an outcome dashboard and a Stripe invoice preview API call exposed through the product UI. Surprises on the monthly invoice are a churn driver.
5. **Feedback loop from outcome to invoice must be under 30 days.** Outcomes that take 90 days to verify (e.g., "did this lead become a customer?") are not viable for RaaS without a proxy metric (e.g., "lead accepted and contacted by sales within 5 days"). Long feedback loops prevent pricing calibration and create cash flow risk.
6. **API is the product. UI is the demo.** Every outcome-generating capability is available as a documented, versioned API endpoint before the UI is built. SDK libraries for primary customer languages (Python, Node, Go) ship with the GA release. Customers who embed your API into their workflows are 3x stickier than UI-only users.

## Decision Rules

**IF** the outcome is not causally attributable to your service alone, **THEN** RaaS is not viable without agreed attribution rules in the contract. "Revenue generated by the customer" is not attributable — too many confounding factors. "Document successfully OCR-processed and returned within SLA" is fully attributable.

**IF** the feedback loop for outcome verification exceeds 30 days, **THEN** identify a proxy outcome with a <7-day feedback loop and price against the proxy, not the lagging outcome. Document the proxy-to-outcome correlation in the contract.

**IF** cost-per-outcome is above 40% of price-per-outcome at any expected volume tier, **THEN** do not launch at that price. Either raise the price, reduce COGS (optimize the pipeline), or switch to an outcome-floor subscription hybrid (minimum monthly commitment + per-outcome above the floor).

**IF** a customer disputes an outcome count, **THEN** the dispute resolution process must produce a resolution within 5 business days using the immutable event log. If you cannot audit the outcome back to a specific event record with a timestamp, the billing architecture is non-compliant for RaaS.

**IF** designing the pricing model, **THEN** default to the outcome-floor subscription hybrid for B2B: minimum monthly commitment (covering your fixed cost floor) + per-outcome pricing above the included allocation. Pure pay-per-outcome pricing exposes you to zero-revenue months when the customer's pipeline is quiet — this is cash flow risk you own, not them.

**IF** a customer's outcome volume drops >30% month-over-month, **THEN** CS must initiate an outcome health review within 5 business days — this is an early churn signal, not a billing issue. CS compensation must be tied to customer outcomes, not just renewal signatures.

**IF** building SDK design, **THEN** the SDK must expose an `OutcomeClient` that handles: event submission with idempotency, local retry with exponential backoff, batch submission for high-volume producers, and a dry-run mode for testing. Never make the customer responsible for idempotency logic.

**IF** a new outcome type is being added to the product, **THEN** go through this gate before pricing it: (1) Can it be measured without customer self-reporting? (2) Is there a unique identifier for each outcome instance? (3) Can it be audited in the event log? All three must be yes.

**NEVER** let the outcome definition be modified after contract signing without a signed amendment. Retroactive changes to outcome definitions are a trust-destroying event and a legal liability. Lock the definition to the contract version.

**NEVER** build outcome measurement inside the same service that generates the outcome. Outcome measurement must be an independent audit trail — ideally a separate service that observes outcome events from a stream, not a flag set by the generating process itself.

## Mental Models

**The Outcome Contract Triangle**
Every RaaS outcome has three attributes that must be agreed upfront: Definition (exactly what constitutes the outcome), Measurement method (how it is detected and recorded), and Audit mechanism (how a customer can verify the count independently). All three corners must be solid before pricing. A weak corner leads to disputes. Most RaaS failures trace to an ambiguous Definition corner — "we delivered value" arguments that neither side can resolve with data.

**The Unit Economics Stack**
Work backward from the outcome price:
```
Price per outcome: $X
  - COGS (infra, model, processing cost): must be < $X * 0.40
  - Gross margin: > $X * 0.60
  - Support + disputes allowance: ~$X * 0.10
  - Net contribution per outcome: > $X * 0.50 at scale
```
Run this model at three volume scenarios: 1K/month, 10K/month, 100K/month. If gross margin improves with volume (economies of scale), RaaS economics are favorable. If it stays flat or degrades (human-in-the-loop bottlenecks), you have a scaling problem before a pricing problem.

**The API-First Forcing Function**
Treat the public API contract as if an adversarial third party is consuming it — no undocumented fields, no breaking changes without versioning, no implicit dependencies on UI session state. This discipline forces clean outcome event schemas, clear idempotency key contracts, and stable outcome type identifiers. The side effect is that your UI becomes a thin consumer of your own API, making your API easier to document and your UI easier to test.

**The Trust Ledger**
In RaaS, every billing interaction either deposits or withdraws from the customer's trust ledger. Deposits: real-time outcome dashboard, accurate invoice preview, proactive anomaly alerts ("your outcome rate dropped this week"). Withdrawals: unexplained invoice line items, outcome counts the customer cannot verify, disputes that take > 5 days to resolve. A high-trust ledger balance is the primary retention mechanism — RaaS customers stay because they believe the outcomes are real, not because switching costs are high.

## Vocabulary

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **RaaS (Results-as-a-Service)** | Pricing model where customers pay per verified outcome delivered (e.g., per lead, per document processed) rather than per seat or per API call. |
| **Outcome definition** | The precise, contractually agreed description of what constitutes one billable outcome instance, including measurement method and audit procedure. |
| **Idempotency key** | A stable, deterministic identifier for an outcome event that prevents double-counting on retry. Typically a content hash of outcome-specific attributes. |
| **Outcome-floor subscription** | Hybrid pricing: fixed monthly minimum (covering cost floor) + per-outcome pricing above the included allocation. Reduces cash flow risk for the vendor. |
| **Cost-per-outcome** | Total variable cost (infrastructure, model, labor) attributable to producing one outcome instance. Must stay below 40% of price-per-outcome for sustainability. |
| **Proxy outcome** | A near-term measurable event that is strongly correlated with the lagging true outcome, used when the feedback loop on the true outcome exceeds 30 days. |
| **Usage event** | Immutable, append-only record of a billable outcome occurrence. The source of truth for invoice generation and dispute resolution. |
| **Attribution** | The causal link establishing that your service produced the outcome, not ambient market conditions or the customer's own efforts. Must be defensible. |
| **Metered billing** | Billing model where charges are calculated from aggregated usage events, typically via Stripe Metered Billing API with usage records reported per billing period. |
| **Outcome dashboard** | Customer-facing UI showing real-time outcome counts, projected invoice, and historical trends. Required for trust; not optional. |
| **Dispute resolution SLA** | Contractual commitment on how long it takes to resolve a customer challenge to an outcome count. Target: 5 business days for RaaS. |
| **ARPO (Average Revenue Per Outcome)** | Revenue per outcome instance; used to track pricing efficiency over time. Rising ARPO with stable volume = successful upsell. Falling ARPO = pricing pressure or mix shift. |

## Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

**Mistake 1: Ambiguous outcome definition that invites disputes**
- **Bad contract language:** "Customer will be charged for each 'qualified lead' generated by the platform." (What is qualified? Who decides? What if the lead never responds?)
- **Fix:** "Customer will be charged for each lead record where: (a) the lead's email address is verified deliverable by the platform's email validation service, (b) the lead has not appeared in the customer's CRM within the prior 90 days, and (c) the lead meets the ICP filters defined in Exhibit A at time of generation. Each such event is recorded in the outcome log with a unique `outcome_id`, `occurred_at` timestamp, and the ICP filter version applied."

**Mistake 2: Outcome measurement owned by the same process that produces the outcome**
- **Bad:** The lead generation service sets `billable = true` on its own output record before writing to the database — the fox guards the henhouse; any bug in the generation service corrupts billing.
- **Fix:** Generation service emits a raw `lead_created` event to a Kafka topic. A separate, independently deployed `OutcomeMeasurementService` consumes the stream, applies the outcome definition rules, and emits a `billable_outcome_recorded` event to a separate append-only ledger. The two services have no shared code paths.

**Mistake 3: No idempotency on usage event ingestion**
- **Bad:** Network timeout causes the outcome reporting client to retry; two identical events are recorded; customer is billed twice for one outcome. Customer disputes invoice; trust is damaged.
- **Fix:** Every event payload includes `idempotency_key: sha256(outcome_type + tenant_id + outcome_entity_id + date_bucket)`. Ingestion endpoint returns 200 on duplicate key without creating a new record. SDK handles retries transparently. Idempotency window: 24 hours.

**Mistake 4: Pricing pure pay-per-outcome with no floor**
- **Bad:** Customer signs pure pay-per-outcome deal. Their marketing team goes quiet in August. Vendor earns $0 in August despite fixed infrastructure costs being unchanged. At 40 customers, this creates unpredictable MRR that breaks financial planning.
- **Fix:** Outcome-floor subscription: "$2,000/month includes 500 outcome units; $4.00 per unit above 500." Customer has a minimum commitment that covers vendor cost floor; vendor has predictable base MRR; customer has incentive to maximize volume to reduce effective per-unit cost.

**Mistake 5: CS team measured on renewal rate instead of customer outcomes**
- **Bad:** CS quota is "retain 90% of ARR." CS spends time on relationship management and renewal paperwork; outcome delivery degradation goes unnoticed until the renewal call, by which point the customer has already decided to churn.
- **Fix:** CS quota is outcome-delivery based: e.g., "each account must maintain >80% of contracted monthly outcome volume." CS team has outcome dashboards per account with 30-day trend alerts. When volume drops >20% for two consecutive weeks, CS triggers an outcome health review before the customer brings it up.

## Good vs. Bad Output

**BAD: Vague outcome contract + mutable usage counter**
```python
# Outcome definition in contract: "per value-add event" (undefined)

# Usage tracking: mutable counter — no audit trail
def record_outcome(tenant_id):
    db.execute(
        "UPDATE tenant_usage SET outcome_count = outcome_count + 1 WHERE tenant_id = ?",
        [tenant_id]
    )
# Cannot replay. Cannot audit. Cannot resolve disputes. Race condition at volume.
```

**GOOD: Precise outcome definition + immutable event ledger + idempotent ingestion**
```python
# Contract defines outcome precisely (see Mistake 1 Fix above)

import hashlib, uuid
from datetime import datetime, timezone

def record_outcome_event(
    tenant_id: str,
    outcome_type: str,
    entity_id: str,       # e.g., lead_id, document_id — the specific instance
    metadata: dict,
) -> str:
    """
    Append an immutable outcome event. Idempotent on (tenant_id, outcome_type, entity_id, day).
    Returns outcome_id. Safe to retry.
    """
    day_bucket = datetime.now(timezone.utc).strftime("%Y-%m-%d")
    idempotency_key = hashlib.sha256(
        f"{outcome_type}:{tenant_id}:{entity_id}:{day_bucket}".encode()
    ).hexdigest()

    outcome_id = str(uuid.uuid4())
    try:
        db.execute(
            """
            INSERT INTO outcome_events
              (outcome_id, tenant_id, outcome_type, entity_id, idempotency_key, occurred_at, metadata)
            VALUES (?, ?, ?, ?, ?, ?, ?)
            ON CONFLICT (idempotency_key) DO NOTHING
            """,
            [outcome_id, tenant_id, outcome_type, entity_id,
             idempotency_key, datetime.now(timezone.utc), json.dumps(metadata)]
        )
    except IntegrityError:
        pass  # duplicate — correct behavior, not an error

    # Report to Stripe Metered Billing (async, non-blocking)
    stripe_reporter.enqueue(tenant_id, outcome_type, idempotency_key)

    return outcome_id


# Stripe metered billing usage record (reported by background worker)
stripe.SubscriptionItem.create_usage_record(
    subscription_item_id,
    quantity=1,
    timestamp=int(occurred_at.timestamp()),
    action="increment",
    idempotency_key=idempotency_key,   # Stripe deduplicates on this key
)
```

## Checklist

- [ ] Outcome definition in contract specifies: exact trigger condition, measurement method, unique identifier structure, and audit procedure
- [ ] Two parties can independently verify outcome counts from the event log — no black-box measurement
- [ ] Cost-per-outcome modeled at 1K / 10K / 100K monthly volume; gross margin >60% at each tier
- [ ] Outcome measurement service is independently deployed from the outcome-generating service — no shared code path
- [ ] Usage events are append-only; idempotency key on every event; ingestion is safe to retry
- [ ] Stripe Metered Billing configured; usage records include idempotency key matching the internal event log
- [ ] Customer-facing outcome dashboard shows real-time count, 30-day trend, and projected invoice (Stripe invoice preview API)
- [ ] Dispute resolution process documented and committed to ≤5 business day SLA in the contract
- [ ] Feedback loop from outcome occurrence to verified billing event is ≤30 days (proxy metric used if lagging)
- [ ] Pricing model includes a monthly floor commitment — pure pay-per-outcome is reviewed and explicitly approved as exception
- [ ] SDK ships with: idempotency handling, retry logic, batch submission, dry-run mode, and usage preview endpoint
- [ ] CS team OKRs tied to customer outcome delivery rate, not solely renewal ARR
