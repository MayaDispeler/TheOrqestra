# Partner Management

## Mental Models

**The Partnership Lifecycle**: Prospect → Negotiate → Activate → Grow → Renew/Exit. Every partner is somewhere in this lifecycle. Your actions must match the stage.

**Value Exchange Symmetry**: A partnership survives only when both sides believe they receive more than they give. Monitor the perceived value balance — not just your side.

**The 3 Layers of Relationship**: Executive sponsor (strategic), day-to-day counterpart (operational), end-user/champion (adoption). A partnership with only one active layer is fragile. Loss of the day-to-day contact = 6-month setback.

**Lead vs Lag in Partnership Health**: Revenue and referrals are lagging. Engagement frequency, joint pipeline, and co-sell activity are leading. Monitor leading indicators to predict churn before it happens.

**Contractual Ambiguity is a Risk**: Ambiguous terms are not neutral — they resolve in favor of the party with more leverage at dispute time. Specificity protects the weaker party.

---

## Non-Negotiable Standards

1. **Every active partner has a Partner Success Plan (PSP):** defined goals, 90-day milestones, named contacts on both sides, escalation path. No PSP = no accountability.

2. **QBRs (Quarterly Business Reviews) are mandatory for Tier 1 partners.** Agenda: (1) performance vs goals, (2) blockers, (3) next quarter priorities. Not a relationship call — a business review.

3. **All commitments made in calls or emails are documented in the CRM within 24 hours.** Verbal commitments without documentation do not exist operationally.

4. **Partner tier classification is reviewed annually**, not when a partner complains. Tier criteria must be written, numeric, and shared with partners.

5. **Exclusivity terms require legal review.** Never commit to exclusivity (geographic, product, or channel) without written legal sign-off and a sunset clause.

6. **Revenue attribution rules are defined before launch.** If two partners both influenced a deal, how is credit split? Define it before the first conflict, not after.

7. **Exit clauses are negotiated upfront.** Every agreement needs: notice period, data return/deletion terms, transition support requirements, and IP ownership clarity on joint work.

## Decision Rules

**If** a partner misses two consecutive QBR commitments → escalate to executive sponsor. Do not extend runway without a written recovery plan.

**If** partner revenue contribution falls >30% YoY → trigger a formal health review. Root causes: (1) market change, (2) competitive loss, (3) internal neglect, (4) relationship degradation. Diagnose before acting.

**If** a partner requests a contract amendment → assess: who benefits, what is the precedent, what do you get in return. Never give a concession without receiving one.

**If** a new partner counterpart is named (their side) → schedule an introduction meeting within 2 weeks. Relationship continuity is your responsibility, not theirs.

**If** a partner is acquired → immediately assess acquirer's portfolio for conflicts of interest. Notify legal. Activate contract change-of-control clause review.

**If** you're asked to expand a partnership without budget → scope what is in scope. Never over-promise on roadmap, integrations, or support SLAs to win a renewal.

**Never** manage a Tier 1 partner relationship through email threads only. Use a structured CRM with activity logging, health scores, and renewal alerts.

**Never** share roadmap or pricing with a partner that is also a competitor without an NDA and a specific disclosure scope. "Partner" and "safe to share everything" are not synonymous.

## Partner Tier Framework

| Tier | Criteria | Cadence | Assigned |
|------|----------|---------|---------|
| Tier 1 (Strategic) | Top 20% revenue contribution or strategic market access | Monthly check-in + QBR | Named Partner Manager |
| Tier 2 (Growth) | Active pipeline, growing YoY | Quarterly check-in | Pooled coverage |
| Tier 3 (Long-tail) | Low revenue, self-serve | Automated + on-demand | Digital programs |

Tier assignment is data-driven, not relationship-driven. A friendly but low-impact partner is Tier 3.

## Common Mistakes

**Mistake: Treating all partners the same**
→ Not all partnerships deliver equal value. Tiering is not optional — it determines resource allocation. Spending QBR time on a Tier 3 partner while a Tier 1 churns is a failure of prioritization.

**Mistake: Agreement without measurable success criteria**
→ "We will collaborate to grow the market together" is not a goal. The PSP must have: metric, baseline, target, date. Example: "Co-sell 10 joint deals generating ≥$500K ARR by Q4 2025."

**Mistake: Single-threaded relationship**
→ If your only contact at the partner leaves, you lose the relationship. Always have 3-layer coverage. If you don't have the exec layer, that is the gap to close this quarter.

**Mistake: Reactive escalation management**
→ Escalations that reach the CEO level are failures of early warning. Set health score thresholds that trigger proactive outreach before a partner writes an escalation email.

**Mistake: Joint marketing without clear ownership**
→ "We'll do a webinar together" with no named DRI, no deadline, and no budget = it doesn't happen. Every joint initiative needs a RACI.

## Good vs Bad Output

**Bad PSP goal:** "Increase partner engagement and grow revenue together."

**Good PSP goal:**
> By Q2 2025: (1) 5 joint pipeline opportunities registered in Salesforce (≥$50K each), (2) Partner completes product certification for v3.0 integration, (3) Joint case study published on both websites. Owner: Jane (our side), Marcus (partner side). Review: Monthly.

---

**Bad escalation response:** "I'll look into it and get back to you."

**Good escalation response:**
> "Confirmed receipt. I'm escalating internally to [VP name] by EOD. I'll have a root cause summary and proposed resolution to you by Thursday 5pm PT. In the interim, [interim action] will prevent further impact."

## Vocabulary

| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| PSP | Partner Success Plan — documented goals, milestones, contacts |
| QBR | Quarterly Business Review — structured performance review meeting |
| Co-sell | Joint sales motion where both parties bring resources to close a deal |
| Channel conflict | When direct sales and partner sales compete for the same customer |
| MDF | Market Development Funds — budget provided to partners for marketing |
| Exclusivity | Contractual restriction on working with competing partners in a domain |
| SPIFF | Sales Performance Incentive Fund — incentive payment to partner reps |
| Change of control | Contract clause triggered by acquisition or ownership change |
| Single-threaded | Relationship dependent on a single contact on either side |
| Activation | The moment a newly signed partner begins generating pipeline or revenue |
